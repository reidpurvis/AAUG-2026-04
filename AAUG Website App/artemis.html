Mission Overview

The First Crewed Lunar Mission in 54 Years

NASA's Artemis II is the first crewed test flight of the Orion spacecraft and Space Launch System (SLS), sending four astronauts on a ~10-day journey around the Moon. Launched on 1 April 2026, it is the first mission to send humans beyond low Earth orbit since Apollo 17 in December 1972.

The crew — Commander Reid Wiseman, Pilot Victor Glover, Mission Specialist Christina Koch (NASA), and Mission Specialist Jeremy Hansen (CSA) — are currently aboard the Orion spacecraft named Integrity, heading back to Earth after completing their historic lunar flyby on 6 April.

During the flyby, the crew surpassed the distance record set by Apollo 13 in 1970, travelling a maximum of 252,760 miles from Earth — approximately 4,105 miles further than any humans had previously ventured.

The mission is a test flight: validating Orion's life support systems, navigation, and deep-space communications with crew aboard for the first time — laying the groundwork for Artemis III, which aims to land humans on the lunar surface.

The Crew

Four Astronauts. One Historic Journey.

The Artemis II crew represents a milestone in diversity and international partnership — including the first woman and first person of colour to travel to the vicinity of the Moon.

RW

Reid Wiseman

Commander
NASA · United States
US Navy test pilot and veteran ISS commander. Selected as Artemis II commander in April 2023. Third spaceflight.
VG

Victor Glover

Pilot
NASA · United States
US Navy pilot and former Crew Dragon mission pilot to the ISS. First Black astronaut to travel to the Moon's vicinity.
CK

Christina Koch

Mission Specialist
NASA · United States
Electrical engineer and research associate who holds the record for the longest single spaceflight by a woman. First woman to travel to the Moon's vicinity.
JH

Jeremy Hansen

Mission Specialist
CSA · Canada
Royal Canadian Air Force fighter pilot. First Canadian to travel beyond low Earth orbit. First non-American to travel to the vicinity of the Moon.

Liftoff — Kennedy Space Center LC-39B
SLS rocket lifts off from Kennedy Space Center. Solar array wings deploy 49 minutes after launch. Successful translunar injection burn at ~7:49 PM EDT sends crew toward the Moon.

2 April 2026 · Day 2
Translunar Injection & Orion Proximity Operations
Crew demonstrates manual manoeuvring of Orion relative to the Interim Cryogenic Propulsion Stage. Enters high Earth orbit then begins trans-lunar coast.

3–4 April 2026 · Days 3–4
En Route to Moon — Systems Checkout
Crew checks life support systems, completes space adaptation activities, CPR demonstrations, and medical device checkouts. Christina Koch photographs Earth from Orion's window. "Rise" the zero-g mascot floats freely in the cabin.
✓
5 April 2026 · Day 5
Enters Moon's Gravitational Sphere of Influence
Crew enters lunar gravitational sphere of influence. Suit demonstrations and final briefing from Mission Control covering flyby objectives and exact lunar lighting conditions.
✓
6 April 2026 · Day 6 — LUNAR FLYBY
Historic Lunar Flyby — Farthest Humans From Earth
Crew passes within 4,070 miles of the lunar surface. Sets new human distance record at 252,760 miles from Earth — surpassing Apollo 13's 53-year-old record by 4,105 miles. Brief comms blackout during far-side pass. Crew photographs the Moon and Earth.

7 April 2026 · Day 7 · TODAY
Return Journey — En Route to Earth
Orion is using Earth's gravity in a free-return trajectory, pulling the spacecraft back toward Earth without requiring a major burn. Crew continues checkout activities and communications with Mission Control.
